The internet wasn’t “invented” in a single moment. It emerged from a chain of practical needs:
resilient communication, efficient routing, and open standards that could survive constant change.

## The seed: networks before the internet

Early computing was isolated. Sharing data meant physical media, scheduled access, and strict
organizational boundaries. The first breakthroughs were not about websites—they were about
**interconnected systems**.

## ARPANET and packet switching

Packet switching split information into chunks, routed them independently, and reassembled them at
the destination. That one concept made a future global network possible.

## TCP/IP: the universal language

When networks needed to talk across incompatible hardware, the solution wasn’t a single vendor—it
was a protocol. TCP/IP became the common contract.

## The web arrives

The World Wide Web turned the internet into something people could browse. URLs, HTML, and HTTP
made knowledge linkable and shareable.

## Why it scaled

It scaled because it was:
- **Decentralized**
- **Standards-driven**
- **Designed for failure**

## What’s next

The next era is less about “being online” and more about **trust**: security, identity, privacy, and
information quality.
